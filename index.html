Colorizing the Prokudin-Gorskii Photo Collection

Automatic Image Alignment and Color Reconstruction

Project Overview

Sergei Mikhailovich Prokudin-Gorskii was a pioneering photographer who, in the early 1900s, traveled across the Russian Empire capturing thousands of color photographs. His technique involved taking three exposures of each scene through red, green, and blue filters. With modern image processing, we can digitally align and combine these separate exposures to recreate his stunning color images.

This project implements an automatic alignment algorithm that takes digitized Prokudin-Gorskii glass plate images and produces vivid color photographs with minimal visual artifacts. The challenge is that the three color channels (Blue, Green, and Red) need to be precisely aligned before stacking them together, as even small misalignments create noticeable color fringing.

Methodology

Alignment Metrics

  • L2 Norm (Euclidean Distance): Measures the pixel-wise difference between images
  • NCC (Normalized Cross-Correlation): Computes dot product between normalized image vectors
  • Both metrics used depending on which gives better results for each image

Single-Scale Alignment

  • Used for low-resolution .jpg images
  • Exhaustive search over displacement window [-15, 15] pixels
  • Fast and effective for smaller images

Multi-Scale Pyramid Alignment

  • Used for high-resolution .tif images
  • Coarse-to-fine pyramid approach
  • Starts at coarsest scale and refines at finer levels
  • Handles large displacements efficiently

Enhancements

  • Automatic Contrast: Rescales intensities for better visual quality

Bells & Whistles: Automatic Contrast Enhancement

One enhancement implemented in this project is automatic contrast adjustment. The raw aligned images often appear washed out or dim because the original glass plates weren't exposed with modern displays in mind. By rescaling the image intensities so that the darkest pixel maps to 0 and the brightest pixel maps to 1 (on each color channel), we can improve the visual quality of the final images.The technique is simple but effective, requiring just a linear rescaling of pixel values after alignment is complete.

Failures

emir.tif: The subject of the image (the emir) appears to be slightly misaligned probably due to them moving in between the shots taken for the different color channels

Results

Single-Scale Alignment Results (Low-Resolution JPEGs)

For the low-resolution .jpg images (cathedral, monastery, and tobolsk), a simple single-scale alignment using exhaustive search over a [-15, 15] pixel window was sufficient. These images are small enough that we can afford to check every possible displacement and pick the one that maximizes the NCC metric. All three images aligned successfully with small offsets, typically under 15 pixels in each direction.


Multi-Scale Pyramid Alignment Results (Low-Resolution JPEGs and High-Resolution TIFFs)

The high-resolution .tif images require a more sophisticated approach. With image dimensions in the thousands of pixels, exhaustive search becomes prohibitively slow. Instead, I implemented a coarse-to-fine pyramid alignment that starts by aligning downsampled versions of the images and progressively refines the alignment at higher resolutions. This approach handles the large displacements (often 100+ pixels) efficiently while maintaining accuracy.

Most images were aligned successfully using the NCC metric. However, for two images (melons.tif and self_portrait.tif), the L2 distance metric produced better alignment results.


Additional Images from the Prokudin-Gorskii Collection

I selected three additional images from the Library of Congress collection to test the robustness of the algorithm: weapons from Borodino, a camel caravan, and a Napoleon portrait. All three images aligned successfully using the NCC metric with pyramid alignment, demonstrating that the approach works well with different types of scenes and subject matter.
